Literalism vs. Allegory: Unpacking the Two Testaments of George Silver

Dec. 13, 2025

The Duel After the Masquerade by Gérôme Jean Leon

George Silver’s teachings come in two distinct Testaments, the Old; Paradoxes of defence, 1599 and the later, Newer testament; Brief Instructions Upon My Paradoxes of Defence, ~1605. Though the later was never published, it was seemingly circulated during his day.

Paradoxes

The first tome, Paradoxes is a lengthy fairly poorly structured polemic against rapier users, chiefly Italians, with sprinklings of some teachings, though often these useful parts are both too cryptic and too fleeting to satisfy any honest reader.

But, if we’re forgiving and look beyond George’s weird pissed up ranting & Tabernacling of simple ideas, instead focusing on the good, squinting very hard we can discover Paradoxes does contain some interesting concepts.

Paradoxes: The Governors

The most enduring concept laid out in this text is the martial sacred tetrad: The Four Governors: Measure, Time, Space, and Strength.

These Governors are the philosophical bedrock of Silver’s entire system. They are the abstract theology that guides the fight. Like the Christian Holy Trinity, they are explicitly defined only in this dense original text, open to extensive allegorical interpretation, and often recited almost ritually by traditionalists. They provide the Why of the fight, but infuriatingly little of the How.

You can gain a copy of both George Silver’s works at the Internet Archive, this includes some useful introductory text and contextualisation.
