Unpacking the Two Testaments of George Silver

Dec. 13, 2025

The Duel After the Masquerade by Gérôme Jean Leon

George Silver’s teachings come in two distinct Testaments, the Old; Paradoxes of defence, 1599 and the later, Newer testament; Brief Instructions Upon My Paradoxes of Defence, ~1605. Though the later was never published, it was seemingly circulated during his day.

Paradoxes

The first tome, Paradoxes is a lengthy fairly poorly structured polemic against rapier users, chiefly Italians, with sprinklings of some teachings, though often these useful parts are both too cryptic and too fleeting to satisfy any honest reader.

It’s a shame Silver squanders most goodwill gained from the small nuggets of offered teaching by consistently going on weird rants. At times the guy truly just seems almost wasted, unleashing some pretty impressive Malbec fueled shitposting:

Now, ô you Italian teachers of Defence, where are your Stocatas, Imbrocatas, Mandritas, Puntas, & Puynta reverjas, Stramions, Paffatas, Carricados, Amazzas, & In-cartatas, & playing with your bodies, remouing with your feet a litle afide, circle wife winding of your bodies,. making of three times with your feet together ,marking with one eye the motion of the aduerfary, & with the o ther eye the aduatage of thrufting?

But, if we’re forgiving and look beyond Silver’s weird foamy rants and exhausting tabernacling of simple ideas, and instead focus on the good, Paradoxes does contain some interesting concepts.

Four Grounds & Governors

Paradoxes refers consistently to Silver’s holy tetrad, The Four Governors as the core of the True Fight. But only comes close to actually defining these at the end of a thirty something long list of True Fight principles, which also includes his Four Grounds individually; (First) Judgement, Space [Distance], Place & Time. These aren’t highlighted in any particular way, they’re just there.

The grounds or Principles of true fight with all maner of weapons.

Firft Judgement, Lyings, Diftance, Directi-on, Pafe, Space, Place, Time, Indirecti-on, Motion, Action, generall and conti-nuall Motion, Progreflion, Regrefion, Trauerfing, and Treading of groundes, Blowes, Thruftes, Faulfes, Doubles, Slipes, Wardes, breakings of Thrufts, Clofings, Gripes, & Wraftlings, Guardant fight, Open fight, Variable fight, and Clofe fight, and foure Gouernours.

Later, Silver probably noticed this after publishing and sought to correct it within Brief Instructions, where he notes missing them out and later goes on to define them pretty explicitly as well as highlight their importance. Whoops.

Bref Inftructions ups my pradoxes of Defence for the true handlyng of all Mann’ of weapons together w the fower grownds & the fower gownors wo governours are left out in my pradoxes w’out the knowledge of ws no Man can fight faf.

[Brief Instructions upon my Paradoxes of Defence for the true handling of all manner of weapons together with the four grounds and the four governors which governors are left out in my paradoxes without the knowledge of which no man can fight safe.] (Greg Lindahl’s transcription)

The four grounds or principals of that true fight at all manner of weapons are these four, viz. 1. judgment, 2. distance, 3. time, 4. place.

The four govenors are defined a bit further on:

The 4 gor nors are thofe j follore.

(1.) The fyrft gou’nor is Judgment wch is to know when can do the tycan hah, go to know by to, goodnes or badnes of his lyinge, what he can do, & when & how h e can p’torme it.

(2.) The fecond gou nor is Meafure. Meafure is the better to know how to make yo fpace true to defend yor felf, or to oftend yo’ enemye

(3. 4.) The third & fourth gou’ners is a twyfold mynd when yo prefs in on yo enemye, for as yõ have a mynd to go forwarde, lo yo muft haue at yt initant a mynd to fij Jackwarde p o any action y’ fhalbe offered or don by yo adu farie.

Again but in the clarity of Greg Lindahl’s transcription:

The 4 governors are those that follow

(1.) The first governor is judgment which is to know when your adversary can reach you, and when not, and when you can do the like to him, and to know by the goodness or badness of his lying, what he can do, and when and how he can perform it.

(2.) The second governor is measure. Measure is the better to know how to make your space true to defend yourself, or to offend your enemy.

(3. 4.) The third and forth governors are a twofold mind when you press in on your enemy, for as you have a mind to go forward, so must you have at that instant a mind to fly backward upon any action that shall be offered or done by your adversary.

You can gain a copy of both George Silver’s works at the Internet Archive, this includes some useful introductory text and contextualisation.
